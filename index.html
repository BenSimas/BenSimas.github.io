Titanofonias

What it is:

Soulslike action RPG.
Souls games were some of my favourite games of all time. And I was very excited to be able to make something of my own.
The player is thrown inside a dwarven dungeon deep underground, and is tasked with seeing themselves through to the end of the dungeon, killing all enemies and bosses in their path.

Systems designed and implemented:

  • Frame-accurate animation-to-logic synchronization, ensuring hit detection, stamina consumption, and state transitions aligned precisely with animation timing.
  • Custom kinematic character controller handling movement, ground detection, slope logic, gravity, and collision resolution without relying on Unity’s built-in controller.
  • Hierarchical player state machine managing locomotion, attack chains, blocking, ranged combat, hit reactions, and dodge rolls with transition rules and interruption handling.
  • Extended the base AI framework to support boss-exclusive mechanics without duplicating core logic.
  • Event-driven audio system handling contextual SFX (footsteps, attacks, hit reactions) and dynamic music transitions between ambient, combat, and boss states using Unity’s AudioMixer.
  • Stamina-based player combat controller featuring dodge i-frames, attack chains and animation-synced hit detection.
  • Modular enemy AI using state-driven behavior systems supporting patrol, chase, attack sequences, stagger reactions, and boss-specific mechanics.

Technical challenges solved:

  • URP shadow atlas overflow by implementing runtime shadow activation based on player proximity, significantly reducing GPU pressure.
  • Designed scalable enemy architecture supporting multiple archetypes and bosses.
  • Systematically tuned per-animation hitbox activation and recovery windows to align damage registration with telegraph frames, improving combat readability and eliminating unfair early/late hit detection.
  • Addressed performance and visual clarity challenges by refining light baking settings, shadow resolution, and static batching to reduce GPU load while preserving atmospheric lighting.

Lessons I took from this project:

  • Learned to design state machines that remain readable and maintainable as feature complexity grows.
  • Significant render pipeline understanding.
  • Developing a custom kinematic controller reinforced how deceptively complex character physics can be, particularly when aiming for stability under edge cases.

Conquest: Raid Warfare

What it is:

My first shipped project. And a big stepping stone to learning the importance of the fundamentals.
For my first game I wanted to make something simple yet complex. And a mixture of RTS and simulation really hit it on the nail.
This is a strategy battle simulator game where players strategically place units in formations before automated combat resolves against an opposing team.

Systems designed and implemented:

  • Custom grid-based placement system with coordinate mapping, tile validation, and visual feedback.
  • Data-driven unit system using ScriptableObjects to allow scalable addition of new units without modifying core combat logic.
  • Ai with combat resolution systems handling targeting logic, attack patterns, attack resolutions and skills.
  • UI with extensive menus, submenus and navigation.
  • JSON-based persistence system for player progression, unlockables, and game state.
  • Performance optimizations to support large numbers of active units.

Technical challenges solved:

  • Identified and resolved performance bottlenecks caused by large numbers of active units by refactoring update loops, reworking expensive UI refreshes, and optimizing shadows and rendering.
  • Balance was a big issue throughout the development. Constant balancing of unit types and their stats and abilities was crucial to keeping the game healthy.

Lessons I took from this project:

  • Importance of system decoupling and clean architecture.
  • Designing data-driven gameplay systems.
